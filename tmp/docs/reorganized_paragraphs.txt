0020	Heading 1	Abstract
0021	Normal	GreenNet is an AI-driven routing simulation and evaluation framework designed to explore how network infrastructures can reduce energy consumption while maintaining acceptable Quality of Service (QoS). As Internet traffic continues to grow, traditional routing approaches prioritize connectivity, shortest paths, and reliability, but they do not explicitly optimize for sustainability. This project addresses that gap by combining network simulation, energy-aware control policies, and intelligent decision-making methods within a unified experimental platform.
0022	Normal	The system models network topologies, traffic scenarios, and routing behavior, and evaluates multiple policies, including traditional always-on routing, heuristic energy-aware control, and reinforcement learning-based optimization. GreenNet measures key performance indicators such as energy consumption, delivered and dropped traffic, delay, latency, carbon impact, and QoS compliance, enabling a structured comparison of routing strategies under realistic and dynamic conditions. In addition to the simulation core, the project includes reproducible evaluation workflows, persistent result storage, and a web-based interface for visualizing scenario outcomes and policy trade-offs.
0023	Normal	The main contribution of GreenNet is a practical and academically grounded framework for studying sustainable networking through AI-assisted routing decisions. Rather than treating energy efficiency as an isolated objective, the project evaluates how energy savings can be achieved responsibly, without unacceptable degradation in service quality. The results demonstrate the value of combining classical networking principles with modern AI techniques to support greener and more transparent network management.
0025	Normal	Keywords: Green networking, energy-aware routing, artificial intelligence, reinforcement learning, network simulation, Quality of Service, sustainable computing, traffic optimization.
0028	Heading 2	Introduction
0029	Normal	The rapid growth of Internet traffic, cloud services, and always-connected digital systems has made computer networks more important than ever. At the same time, this expansion has increased the energy consumption of networking infrastructure, including routers, switches, and communication links. Although modern networks are typically designed to maximize performance, reliability, and availability, they are often operated in ways that are not energy-efficient, especially during periods of low or fluctuating demand.
0030	Normal	Traditional routing approaches prioritize metrics such as shortest path, link cost, and fast convergence, but they do not explicitly consider sustainability or energy usage as optimization goals. As a result, network components often remain active even when traffic conditions would allow more efficient configurations. This creates an important opportunity for research into smarter routing strategies that can reduce energy consumption without causing unacceptable degradation in Quality of Service (QoS).
0031	Normal	GreenNet was developed in response to this challenge. The project presents an AI-driven routing simulation and evaluation framework that investigates how intelligent control policies can improve the energy efficiency of network operation. Instead of relying only on fixed traditional routing logic, GreenNet compares multiple approaches, including always-on routing, heuristic energy-aware control, and reinforcement learning-based decision-making, within a unified experimental platform. The system is designed to simulate realistic network topologies and traffic scenarios, measure both performance and sustainability-related metrics, and visualize the trade-offs between energy savings and service quality.
0032	Normal	The broader motivation behind this work is the growing need for sustainable computing solutions. As digital infrastructure continues to expand globally, improving the efficiency of network operation is no longer only a technical issue, but also an environmental one. By combining networking principles with artificial intelligence techniques, GreenNet aims to demonstrate that network management can become both more adaptive and more responsible.
0033	Normal	This thesis explores the design, implementation, and evaluation of GreenNet as a practical framework for sustainable routing research. It focuses on whether AI-assisted routing policies can reduce simulated network energy consumption while preserving acceptable performance, thereby contributing to the wider goal of greener and more efficient computing systems.

0063	Heading 2	Literature Review
0064	Normal	The increasing energy demand of digital infrastructure has made sustainable networking an important research area in modern computing. As Internet usage, cloud services, and connected devices continue to expand, network infrastructures consume growing amounts of energy, creating both economic and environmental concerns. Traditional routing systems have historically been designed to optimize connectivity, reliability, and performance, but they have not treated energy efficiency as a primary design objective. This gap has encouraged the development of green networking approaches that attempt to reduce power consumption while maintaining acceptable service quality.
0065	Normal	One major area of related work focuses on traditional routing protocols and their limitations. Classical protocols such as RIP and OSPF are designed around metrics like hop count, shortest path, and link cost. These protocols are highly effective in ensuring reachability and stable packet delivery, but they do not explicitly model energy usage in their routing decisions. As a result, routers, links, and other networking components often remain active regardless of actual traffic demand. This creates inefficiencies, especially during low-utilization periods, where some parts of the network could theoretically operate in lower-power states without significantly affecting performance.
0066	Normal	A second major stream of research concerns green networking and energy-aware routing. Researchers in this area have explored strategies such as traffic consolidation, adaptive link activation, sleep modes for underutilized devices, and optimization-based routing decisions. The main idea behind these approaches is that energy can be saved by concentrating traffic on fewer active paths and temporarily reducing the activity of unused network elements. However, many of these methods are either static, highly simplified, or difficult to apply in real-time environments. In practice, energy-aware routing must balance efficiency with strict performance requirements, since overly aggressive power-saving decisions can increase delay, packet loss, or instability.
0067	Normal	Artificial intelligence has emerged as a promising solution to this challenge. In networking, AI and machine learning have been applied to traffic prediction, anomaly detection, congestion control, and adaptive routing. Forecasting models can help estimate future traffic demand, allowing the system to make proactive decisions rather than reacting only after congestion or underutilization occurs. Reinforcement learning is particularly relevant because it enables an agent to learn control policies through repeated interaction with an environment. Instead of following fixed rules, an RL-based routing strategy can adapt to changing network states and optimize long-term trade-offs between energy savings and Quality of Service. This makes AI-based routing especially suitable for dynamic and uncertain traffic conditions.
0068	Normal	Simulation platforms also play an important role in networking research. High-fidelity simulators such as ns-3 and OMNeT++ provide powerful environments for protocol analysis, but they can be more complex and slower to integrate with machine learning workflows. In contrast, lightweight graph-based simulation frameworks offer flexibility, transparency, and fast experimentation, making them attractive for research projects that combine networking with AI. For a project such as GreenNet, simulation is not only a technical implementation tool but also a research instrument that supports reproducible experimentation, comparative evaluation, and systematic testing across multiple scenarios.
0069	Normal	Another important perspective in the literature is sustainability in computing. Sustainable computing emphasizes the reduction of environmental impact through efficient system design, responsible resource usage, and carbon-aware decision-making. Within this context, energy-efficient networking is not only a performance optimization problem, but also part of a broader ethical and environmental responsibility. Systems that reduce energy usage must still protect fairness, reliability, and service quality, especially when they affect critical digital infrastructure.
0070	Normal	Despite the progress in these areas, there remains a gap between theoretical green networking strategies and practical, accessible frameworks for evaluating them. Many studies focus either on optimization models, isolated AI methods, or highly specialized environments. Fewer projects combine simulation, AI-driven control, structured evaluation, and user-facing visualization into one coherent platform. GreenNet addresses this gap by providing a unified framework for modeling network behavior, comparing routing policies, and studying the trade-offs between energy efficiency and service quality in a reproducible and understandable way.
0071	Normal	Overall, the literature suggests that sustainable networking is both technically relevant and socially important. Traditional routing methods provide a strong operational foundation, but they are limited in terms of energy awareness. Green networking research introduces mechanisms for reducing power usage, while AI adds adaptability and decision-making intelligence. GreenNet builds on these foundations by integrating them into a single experimental framework aimed at exploring practical and responsible approaches to greener network management.
0073	Heading 2	Methodology
0074	Normal	This thesis follows a design-and-evaluation methodology centered on the development of a simulation-based framework for studying energy-aware network routing. The methodological approach combines system design, implementation, controlled experimentation, and comparative evaluation in order to investigate how intelligent routing policies affect both energy consumption and service quality.
0075	Normal	At the core of the methodology is the development of GreenNet as a configurable network simulation and evaluation environment. The framework is designed to model network topologies, traffic conditions, routing strategies, and policy decisions in a controlled and reproducible way. Rather than testing routing approaches directly on real-world infrastructure, the project uses simulation to create a safe and flexible environment for experimentation. This makes it possible to compare multiple policies across different scenarios while maintaining consistency in topology, traffic demand, and performance metrics.
0076	Normal	The first methodological step involves modeling the network environment. Different network topologies and traffic scenarios are defined to represent varied operating conditions, including normal traffic patterns, fluctuating demand, and more dynamic workloads. These scenarios provide the basis for evaluating how routing policies behave under different levels of utilization and stress. The system measures multiple output variables, including energy consumption, delivered traffic, dropped traffic, delay, latency, carbon-related impact, and Quality of Service (QoS) compliance.
0077	Normal	The second step involves implementing and comparing different routing and control strategies. GreenNet includes a traditional always-on routing baseline, an energy-aware heuristic approach, and an AI-assisted strategy based on reinforcement learning. These policies are evaluated under the same simulated conditions to ensure fair comparison. The baseline provides a reference point for conventional network operation, while the heuristic and AI-based approaches are used to test whether more adaptive decision-making can improve sustainability outcomes.
0078	Normal	The third methodological component focuses on data collection and experiment execution. For each run, GreenNet records structured metrics and summaries that describe both performance and energy-related behavior over time. These records support reproducibility and make it possible to aggregate results across multiple scenarios, policies, and random seeds. The use of consistent experiment workflows ensures that comparisons are based on repeatable procedures rather than isolated observations.
0079	Normal	Another important part of the methodology is evaluation and interpretation. The project does not consider energy reduction alone as evidence of success. Instead, the evaluation framework emphasizes trade-offs between energy savings and service quality. A routing policy is considered useful only if it reduces energy usage without causing unacceptable degradation in network performance. For this reason, metrics such as delay, dropped traffic, and QoS violations are treated as essential evaluation criteria alongside energy and carbon-related measures.
0080	Normal	Finally, the methodology includes a reporting and visualization layer. The results of the simulation and evaluation process are organized into structured summaries and presented through a web-based interface and reproducible output artifacts. This improves transparency and helps demonstrate the practical implications of the tested policies. By combining implementation, simulation, experimentation, and analysis, the methodology provides a coherent foundation for examining sustainable routing strategies in a rigorous and academically relevant way.
0082	Heading 2	System Architecture
0083	Normal	GreenNet is designed as a modular system architecture that combines network simulation, policy-driven control, experiment evaluation, persistent result storage, and visualization. The architecture is structured to support both experimentation and analysis, allowing different routing strategies to be tested under controlled conditions while keeping outputs reproducible and easy to interpret.
0084	Normal	At the center of the system is the simulation core. This component models the network as a topology of interconnected nodes and links and executes traffic flows across that topology according to the selected routing and control policy. The simulator is responsible for processing the state of the network over time, applying policy decisions, and computing the resulting performance and energy metrics. Through this design, GreenNet can represent how different routing strategies affect both network behavior and resource usage under changing conditions.
0085	Normal	The traffic generation layer provides the input workload for the simulator. It defines how demand enters the network over time and supports the creation of different traffic scenarios, such as stable demand, fluctuating load, or sudden changes in utilization. This layer is important because routing policies must be evaluated not only under ideal conditions, but also under more realistic and dynamic traffic patterns. By varying traffic scenarios, the system can test whether a policy remains effective and stable across multiple forms of demand.
0086	Normal	Above the simulation layer is the policy control layer. This component determines how routing and energy-related decisions are made during each run. GreenNet supports multiple policy types, including a traditional always-on baseline, a heuristic energy-aware strategy, and an AI-assisted approach based on reinforcement learning. The policy layer interacts with the simulator by observing network conditions and selecting actions that influence how the network operates. This separation between simulation and policy logic makes the system easier to extend, compare, and evaluate.
0087	Normal	Another major component of the architecture is the evaluation and metrics layer. This layer collects results from each simulation run and transforms them into structured performance indicators. These include energy consumption, delivered traffic, dropped traffic, delay, latency, carbon-related impact, and Quality of Service compliance. The evaluation layer supports both per-run analysis and aggregated comparison across multiple scenarios and policies. This is essential for the thesis, since the goal is not only to run simulations, but also to produce interpretable evidence about the trade-offs between sustainability and performance.
0088	Normal	To support reproducibility and traceability, GreenNet also includes a persistence and logging layer. This part of the architecture stores experiment metadata, run summaries, and other structured outputs that describe how each simulation was executed and what results were obtained. Persistent storage ensures that results can be reviewed, compared, and reused later without relying only on temporary in-memory execution. It also supports the production of final summaries and report-ready artifacts.
0089	Normal	The backend service layer acts as the bridge between the simulation system and the user-facing interface. It is responsible for exposing experiment data, summaries, and comparison results through a structured API. This makes it possible for external interfaces to request simulation outputs, load evaluation results, and present them in a clear way. The backend therefore plays a central role in connecting the computational part of the system with the presentation and reporting layer.
0090	Normal	The final component is the visualization layer, implemented as a web-based interface for displaying results and comparisons. This layer allows users to inspect experiment outcomes, analyze trade-offs across policies, and understand the overall performance of the system. Rather than treating visualization as a separate add-on, GreenNet integrates it into the architecture as part of the overall research workflow. This makes the project not only a simulator, but also a complete evaluation framework that supports interpretation and communication of findings.
0091	Normal	Overall, the system architecture of GreenNet is built around modularity, reproducibility, and comparative analysis. Each component has a distinct role, but all components work together to support the main purpose of the project: evaluating whether intelligent routing strategies can improve network energy efficiency while preserving acceptable service quality.

0111	Heading 2	Technologies and Tools Used
0112	Normal	GreenNet was developed using a combination of technologies that support network simulation, AI-based decision-making, backend communication, data persistence, and visualization. Since the project is not only a theoretical study but also a working software system, the choice of tools was driven by practicality, integration, and suitability for experimentation.
0113	Normal	The main programming language used in the project is Python. Python was selected because it provides a strong and mature ecosystem for scientific computing, simulation, machine learning, and API development. In GreenNet, Python serves as the foundation of the entire backend and simulation workflow. The core logic of the simulator, the experiment runners, the policy evaluation pipeline, data aggregation scripts, and the API layer are all built around Python. This made it possible to keep the analytical, algorithmic, and systems components within one coherent environment instead of splitting the project across unrelated technologies.
0114	Normal	For the network representation itself, GreenNet relies on a graph-based modeling approach, where routers, switches, or network devices are represented as nodes and communication links are represented as edges. A key library used for this purpose is NetworkX, which is well suited for constructing and manipulating network topologies, computing routes, and testing path-related behavior. NetworkX made it easier to implement topology-aware routing experiments, shortest-path behavior, and other routing-related logic without needing to build graph operations from scratch. This was especially useful during the prototyping and evaluation phases, where transparency and fast iteration were more important than extremely low-level simulation detail.
0115	Normal	To support numerical computation and structured data handling, the project uses NumPy and pandas. NumPy is useful for efficient array-based operations and repeated metric calculations inside simulation and evaluation workflows. pandas is used for organizing experiment results, aggregating outputs across multiple runs, and generating structured summaries suitable for later analysis. These libraries are particularly important in a project like GreenNet because the value of the system depends not only on running simulations, but also on comparing policies systematically across scenarios, seeds, and evaluation criteria.
0116	Normal	The artificial intelligence part of GreenNet is implemented through a combination of machine learning and reinforcement learning tools. The project design explicitly includes scikit-learn, PyTorch, and Stable-Baselines3 as the main AI-related technologies. Scikit-learn is suitable for lightweight machine learning tasks and structured predictive components, especially when a simpler model is sufficient. PyTorch provides the flexibility needed for more advanced learning-based components and integrates naturally with reinforcement learning workflows. Stable-Baselines3 is particularly important in GreenNet because it offers an established framework for training and evaluating reinforcement learning agents such as PPO. Since one of the central goals of the project is to compare conventional routing behavior with AI-assisted adaptive control, these tools are essential to the technical identity of the system.
0117	Normal	For the web service and system integration layer, GreenNet uses FastAPI. This technology is responsible for exposing simulation outputs, summaries, run metadata, and comparison data through backend endpoints that can be consumed by the user interface. FastAPI was an appropriate choice because it is lightweight, modern, and well suited for Python-based systems that need structured APIs with relatively low implementation overhead. In GreenNet, the backend is not just an optional addition; it is the component that connects the simulation and evaluation logic to the frontend visualization layer.
0118	Normal	On the frontend side, the project includes a web-based dashboard built around a modern JavaScript framework approach, described in the project material as React/Next.js for the dashboard layer. In practical terms, this frontend is used to visualize key performance indicators such as energy consumption, traffic delivery, latency, and policy comparison results. The dashboard is important because GreenNet is meant to be demonstrable and interpretable, not just executable. Instead of leaving the system at the level of raw logs and CSV outputs, the frontend helps transform technical results into a clearer analytical presentation that is more suitable for reviewers, supervisors, and project evaluation.
0119	Normal	For data persistence, GreenNet uses SQLite and also considers PostgreSQL as part of the broader system design. SQLite is especially useful during development and local experimentation because it requires minimal setup while still supporting structured storage of runs, summaries, and experiment-related records. A routing experiment is only useful academically if its conditions and outputs can be stored, revisited, and compared later in a consistent way.

Without version control, managing changes across these layers would have been error-prone and difficult to reproduce.
0122	Normal	Overall, the technologies used in GreenNet reflect the interdisciplinary nature of the project. Python and its scientific ecosystem provide the computational backbone, NetworkX supports network modeling, NumPy and pandas handle data processing, PyTorch and Stable-Baselines3 enable AI-based control, FastAPI connects the backend services, SQLite/PostgreSQL support persistence, and the React-based frontend provides visualization. 0124	Heading 2	Challenges and Limitations
0125	p1	The development of GreenNet involved several technical and methodological challenges that influenced both the implementation process and the scope of the final system. Since the project combines networking, simulation, artificial intelligence, backend integration, and visualization within a single framework, one of the main difficulties was maintaining a balance between realism, complexity, and feasibility. The project was designed to be academically meaningful and technically functional, but like most research-oriented systems, it operates under a number of practical limitations.
0126	p1	One of the main challenges was the nature of the simulation environment itself. GreenNet is intended to study energy-aware routing decisions in a controlled and reproducible way, which makes simulation an appropriate methodology. However, a simulator is still an abstraction of real network behavior. In practice, real-world networks are affected by many additional factors, including protocol overheads, hardware-specific behavior, vendor-dependent energy characteristics, dynamic failures, background control traffic, and deployment constraints that are difficult to reproduce fully in a lightweight experimental platform. As a result, the conclusions drawn from GreenNet should be interpreted as simulation-based findings rather than direct claims about immediate production deployment.
0127	p1	A related limitation concerns the modeling of energy consumption. In order to make policy comparison possible, GreenNet uses an explicit energy model to estimate the effect of routing and power-state decisions on network resource usage. While this is necessary for evaluating sustainability-related outcomes, any such model inevitably simplifies reality. Real networking equipment does not always behave in a perfectly linear or easily predictable way with respect to utilization, sleep states, or transition costs. Some routers and links may consume a large share of their power even when under low load, while others may have different switching penalties or hardware constraints. Therefore, even though GreenNet provides a structured and technically defensible way to compare policies, its energy measurements remain model-based estimates rather than direct physical measurements from deployed infrastructure.
0128	p1	Another important challenge was maintaining the balance between energy savings and Quality of Service. It is relatively easy to reduce simulated energy consumption if a system aggressively disables links, concentrates traffic without sufficient safeguards, or ignores performance degradation. The real difficulty lies in reducing energy usage without causing unacceptable levels of packet drops, excessive delay, instability, or QoS violations. This trade-off is central to the project and also one of its biggest design challenges. Any energy-aware system can appear successful if it is judged only by lower consumption, but such an evaluation would be misleading. For this reason, GreenNet had to be designed around multi-metric comparison, where performance-related indicators remain just as important as sustainability-related ones.
0129	p1	The AI component, especially reinforcement learning, introduces its own limitations as well. Reinforcement learning is attractive because it can adapt decisions to changing network states, but it is also sensitive to reward design, scenario selection, and training stability. An RL policy may perform well in scenarios similar to those seen during training, yet behave less reliably under unfamiliar conditions. It may also learn strategies that optimize the defined reward mathematically without fully reflecting the practical intent of the system. This creates an important methodological challenge: the success of an AI-based routing policy depends not only on the algorithm itself, but also on the quality of the environment design, the state representation, the reward structure, and the diversity of training and evaluation scenarios. In other words, RL does not automatically produce an intelligent solution; it produces a policy that is only as meaningful as the experimental setup behind it.
0130	p1	A further practical limitation was the amount of time available for model training and repeated tuning. Reinforcement learning methods typically require extensive experimentation, multiple retraining cycles, and broad scenario exposure before they approach a stable and well-optimized policy. Within the scope of this capstone project, the PPO agent was trained and evaluated under realistic academic time constraints. This means that the final PPO results should be interpreted as those of a time-bounded and carefully implemented learning-based policy, not as the maximum performance that reinforcement learning could theoretically achieve in this problem setting. This limitation is especially important when interpreting the modest size of the final energy gains.
0131	p1	Traffic modeling was another limitation in the project. GreenNet supports scenario-based experimentation through synthetic or controlled traffic patterns, which is useful for reproducibility and structured comparison. However, synthetic traffic can only approximate the complexity of real network demand. Real traffic often contains irregular bursts, long-range temporal dependencies, application-specific behavior, and patterns driven by human activity or infrastructure events that are difficult to capture fully in a simplified simulator. Because of this, GreenNet can demonstrate how routing policies behave under representative conditions, but it cannot claim to replicate the full unpredictability of operational Internet-scale environments.
0132	p1	Topology realism presents a similar issue. The project is able to model different network structures and compare policy behavior across them, but these topologies are still simplified representations. Real backbone, ISP, enterprise, or data-center networks often include additional constraints such as routing policy rules, heterogeneous hardware capacities, resilience requirements, maintenance windows, and operational restrictions. A research project at this scale cannot fully replicate all such conditions. This means that the architectural and policy insights produced by GreenNet are valuable for analysis and experimentation, but they should not be interpreted as complete substitutes for real network engineering validation.
0133	p1	From a software engineering perspective, another challenge was integrating multiple subsystems into one coherent platform. GreenNet includes simulation logic, policy control, evaluation workflows, persistence, backend APIs, and frontend visualization. Making these layers work together consistently required careful structuring of the codebase and output formats. A change in the simulation layer could affect evaluation summaries; changes in the data schema could affect API responses; and reporting changes could impact how results were interpreted in the dashboard. This kind of integration challenge is common in interdisciplinary software projects, and it influenced the pace and complexity of development.
0134	p1	There is also a practical limitation related to project scope. GreenNet aims to provide a complete research-oriented framework, but it is still developed within the time and scale constraints of an academic capstone project. For that reason, some aspects had to be prioritized over others. The project focuses more strongly on simulation-based evaluation, structured comparison, and reproducible experimentation than on production-grade deployment concerns such as distributed orchestration, hardware integration, real router control interfaces, or very large-scale operational benchmarking. 0137	Heading 2	Evaluation Strategy
0138	Normal	The evaluation strategy of GreenNet is designed to determine whether energy-aware and AI-assisted routing policies can improve network sustainability without causing unacceptable degradation in Quality of Service (QoS). Since the project is centered on comparative analysis rather than only system implementation, evaluation plays a central role in the overall research design. The purpose of the evaluation is not simply to observe whether a policy reduces energy consumption, but to assess whether it does so in a technically responsible and reproducible manner.
0139	Normal	The core principle of the evaluation strategy is controlled comparison. All routing and control policies are evaluated under the same simulated conditions so that differences in outcome can be attributed to policy behavior rather than inconsistent experimental setup. This means that topology conditions, traffic scenarios, and execution settings must remain comparable across runs. The traditional always-on baseline serves as the primary reference point, while heuristic energy-aware control and AI-based policies are evaluated against it in order to determine whether more adaptive approaches produce better trade-offs between sustainability and performance.
0140	Normal	A second important element of the strategy is scenario diversity. A routing policy cannot be judged fairly on the basis of a single traffic pattern or one simplified network condition. For this reason, GreenNet is intended to be evaluated across multiple scenarios that reflect different forms of network demand, such as stable load, fluctuating traffic, and more dynamic or stress-oriented conditions. This allows the system to test whether a policy performs consistently or whether its apparent success depends too heavily on a narrow set of favorable circumstances. The use of varied scenarios is particularly important for AI-based approaches, which may perform well in familiar conditions but less reliably in unfamiliar or rapidly changing ones.
0141	Normal	The evaluation also relies on multi-metric measurement. In GreenNet, success is not defined by energy savings alone. A policy that lowers energy consumption but significantly increases latency, packet drops, or QoS violations cannot be considered effective in a meaningful networking context. For this reason, the project evaluates several categories of metrics together. These include sustainability-related metrics such as total energy consumption and carbon-related impact, as well as performance-related metrics such as delivered traffic, dropped traffic, average delay, latency, and QoS compliance. Considering these metrics together makes it possible to identify whether a routing strategy achieves balanced improvement or whether it simply shifts cost from one dimension to another.
0142	Normal	Another key part of the evaluation strategy is reproducibility. In academic software projects, one successful run is not enough to support a strong conclusion. GreenNet therefore uses a structured workflow in which experiment configurations, summaries, and outputs are stored in a consistent form. This makes it possible to repeat runs, compare policies under the same settings, and aggregate observations across multiple executions. Reproducibility is especially important when AI-based methods are involved, since learning-based policies may be influenced by randomness, initialization, or scenario ordering. A reproducible evaluation process helps reduce the risk of drawing conclusions from isolated or unstable outcomes.
0143	Normal	The strategy also includes aggregation and comparative reporting. Instead of evaluating each run in isolation, GreenNet is designed to summarize results across policies, scenarios, and repeated executions. This allows broader patterns to be identified. For example, a policy may perform very well in low-load conditions but poorly under bursty traffic, or it may reduce energy effectively while introducing unacceptable instability. Aggregated comparison is therefore essential for moving from individual observations to more reliable research conclusions.
0144	Normal	A further aspect of the evaluation strategy is the interpretation of trade-offs. Because GreenNet operates at the intersection of sustainability and network performance, the project is not based on the assumption that one policy will dominate every metric at once. In practice, some energy-saving strategies may slightly increase delay, while some performance-preserving strategies may consume more energy. The evaluation is therefore designed to identify whether the trade-offs remain acceptable, not whether every metric improves simultaneously. This is a more realistic and academically honest way to assess intelligent routing policies.
0145	Normal	Finally, the evaluation strategy supports the broader research questions of the thesis. It is structured to determine whether AI-assisted routing provides measurable practical value beyond conventional routing approaches, whether such improvements remain stable across different operating conditions, and whether GreenNet can function as a reproducible framework for studying sustainable networking decisions. In this sense, the evaluation strategy is not only a way of testing the software, but also the main mechanism through which the thesis answers its research questions.
0146	Normal	Overall, the evaluation strategy of GreenNet is built on controlled comparison, scenario diversity, multi-metric analysis, reproducibility, and careful interpretation of trade-offs. This approach ensures that the project is assessed in a rigorous and technically meaningful way, even before the final experimental results are fully completed.

0173	Heading 2	Why the Hypothesis Was Not Fully Met
0174	p1	The original working expectation of this project was that AI-assisted routing would achieve meaningful energy savings while keeping performance degradation within acceptable limits. The final benchmark confirmed the overall direction of this expectation, but it did not provide full confirmation of the thesis hypothesis. This outcome is important to interpret carefully, because understanding why the gains remained modest is itself a meaningful research result.
0175	p1	Several factors contributed to this outcome. First, the simulation environment uses a lightweight graph-based model in which the difference in energy between active and inactive links is constrained by the chosen power-model assumptions. Second, the PPO policy was trained with a reward structure that strongly penalizes QoS degradation, which encourages conservative behavior and limits aggressive link deactivation. This is an appropriate design choice for a responsible energy-aware system, but it also reduces the maximum energy savings that the policy is likely to realize. Third, reinforcement learning requires substantial training time, repeated tuning cycles, and broad scenario exposure before it approaches a stable and well-generalized policy. Within the scope of this project, the PPO model was trained under limited academic time and experimentation constraints, so the final policy should be interpreted as a time-bounded implementation rather than as a fully optimized upper bound of what reinforcement learning could achieve in this problem setting. Finally, the relatively small and controlled topologies used in the official benchmark provide less room for optimization than larger and more heterogeneous network environments would.
0176	p1	These factors do not reduce the value of the project. Rather, they define the proper boundaries within which the findings should be interpreted. GreenNet was not intended to solve sustainable networking in a final or production-ready sense. Its purpose was to create a structured, transparent, and technically credible framework for studying the problem. In that respect, the project achieved its central aim.
