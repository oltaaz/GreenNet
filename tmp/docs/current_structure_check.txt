0020	Heading 1	Abstract
0021	Normal	GreenNet is a simulation-based framework for studying whether energy-aware link-state control can reduce network energy use without causing unacceptable degradation in Quality of Service (QoS). The project combines a graph-based network simulator, configurable traffic scenarios, an All-On controller, a heuristic controller, and a PPO-based hybrid controller within one benchmarkable environment.
0022	Normal	Each GreenNet run records delivered traffic, dropped traffic, average delay, path latency, estimated energy use, carbon-related output, and controller actions, then stores those artifacts for later comparison. The framework also exposes results through an API and web interface so that evaluation is inspectable rather than dependent on ad hoc screenshots or isolated logs.
0023	Normal	The final thesis contribution is therefore methodological as much as algorithmic. GreenNet shows that sustainable-networking claims can be tested in a reproducible way: the heuristic controller remains the strongest overall policy in the final benchmark, while the PPO-based controller is the strongest AI-based policy and shows only modest gains under selected conditions rather than a decisive routing breakthrough.
0025	Normal	Keywords: Green networking, energy-aware routing, artificial intelligence, reinforcement learning, network simulation, Quality of Service, sustainable computing, traffic optimization.
0028	Heading 2	Introduction
0029	Normal	Network traffic, cloud workloads, and continuously available digital services have increased the operational importance of communication networks while also increasing the electricity demand associated with routers, switches, links, and supporting infrastructure (Gupta & Singh, 2003; IEA, 2022). In that setting, energy efficiency is not a peripheral optimization problem. It directly affects the cost, sustainability, and operational footprint of digital infrastructure.
0030	Normal	Conventional link-state routing was not designed around that objective. Protocols such as OSPF optimize forwarding around reachability, shortest-path cost, and convergence behavior rather than the possibility of placing underutilized links or devices into lower-power states (Moy, 1998). As green-networking research has repeatedly shown, that leaves substantial inefficiency during calm or uneven demand periods because the network often remains provisioned as if peak conditions were permanent (Bianzino et al., 2012; Gupta & Singh, 2003).
0031	Normal	GreenNet addresses this problem at the controller level. Instead of attempting to replace routing with a black-box learner, the project keeps a static shortest-path or OSPF-like forwarding baseline and studies whether link-state control can reduce energy use while preserving service quality. The implemented comparison centers on three policies: an All-On controller that leaves all links active, a heuristic controller that deactivates links conservatively, and a PPO-based hybrid controller whose learned proposals are filtered by explicit safety logic.
0032	Normal	That framing matters academically. The thesis is not claiming that AI has already solved sustainable routing; it is asking whether a carefully instrumented simulator can reveal when adaptive control helps, when it does not, and how those outcomes should be judged against QoS constraints and explicit acceptance thresholds. This keeps the sustainability question tied to measurable networking behavior rather than to broad claims about AI capability (Bianzino et al., 2012; IEA, 2022).
0033	Normal	The remainder of the thesis documents GreenNet as both a software system and an evaluation method. It explains how topologies, traffic, policies, metrics, persistence, and reporting were implemented, and then uses that structure to interpret a conservative final result: the project succeeds most clearly as a reproducible comparative framework for sustainable-networking experiments.
0036	Heading 2	Objectives
0037	Normal	The main objective of this thesis is to design, implement, and evaluate GreenNet as a reproducible framework for energy-aware network control. Rather than proposing a single algorithm in isolation, the project investigates whether configurable simulation, disciplined benchmarking, and explicit QoS constraints can support credible comparison between a conventional All-On controller, a heuristic controller, and a PPO-based hybrid controller.
0038	Normal	The specific objectives of the project are as follows:
0039	List Paragraph	To develop a configurable network simulation environment capable of modeling different topologies, traffic scenarios, and routing behaviors.
0040	List Paragraph	To implement and compare multiple control strategies, including an All-On controller, a heuristic controller, and a PPO-based hybrid controller that combines learned action proposals with rule-based safety constraints.
0041	List Paragraph	To evaluate network behavior using both sustainability-related and performance-related metrics, including energy consumption, delivered traffic, dropped traffic, average delay, path latency, carbon-related output, and QoS compliance.
0042	List Paragraph	To create a reproducible experimental workflow that supports consistent benchmarking across scenarios, seeds, locked evaluation settings, and stored result artifacts.
0043	List Paragraph	To provide a clear visualization and reporting layer through a web-based interface and structured result summaries, making the analysis of policy trade-offs easier to understand.
0044	List Paragraph	To examine whether AI-assisted decision-making can offer a practical and responsible approach to greener network management without causing unacceptable performance degradation.
0045	Normal	Taken together, these objectives position GreenNet as a thesis-specific research platform: it is intended to show not only whether limited energy savings are possible, but also how such claims can be defended, repeated, and interpreted responsibly.
0048	Heading 2	Research Questions and Hypothesis
0049	Normal	This thesis is guided by the broader problem of how network operation can become more energy-aware without crossing unacceptable QoS boundaries. The project therefore asks questions about both controller performance and evaluation method.
0050	Normal	Research Question 1:
0051	Normal	How can intelligent routing and control strategies reduce network energy consumption while maintaining acceptable Quality of Service (QoS)?
0052	Normal	This question is operationalized in GreenNet through explicit comparison of energy use, delivered traffic, dropped traffic, delay, path latency, and QoS threshold compliance. It asks whether link-state control can save energy without simply shifting cost into packet loss or delay.
0053	Normal	Research Question 2:
0054	Normal	How do AI-based approaches compare with the All-On controller and the heuristic controller under different traffic and topology conditions?
0055	Normal	In GreenNet, this comparison is not between abstract algorithm labels but between concrete implementations executed under the same simulator, traffic scenarios, and reporting pipeline. The question therefore tests whether the PPO-based hybrid controller produces a better trade-off than the All-On and heuristic controllers, not whether reinforcement learning is generically promising in principle.
0056	Normal	Research Question 3:
0057	Normal	Can a simulation-driven framework provide a practical and reproducible way to evaluate sustainable routing strategies?
0058	Normal	This question addresses the thesis contribution beyond raw benchmark numbers. A useful result in GreenNet must be repeatable from stored configurations, comparable across policies and scenarios, and interpretable through fixed acceptance logic rather than selective screenshot-level evidence.
0059	Normal	Hypothesis:
0060	Normal	AI-assisted control strategies can reduce simulated network energy consumption relative to an All-On network configuration while maintaining acceptable QoS across multiple traffic scenarios and topology realizations.
0061	Normal	In GreenNet, this expectation is deliberately narrower than a claim that reinforcement learning will outperform every non-AI baseline. The working hypothesis is that adaptive control, especially in the PPO-based controller, may achieve modest but defensible savings under some operating conditions while remaining within explicit QoS limits.
0063	Heading 2	Literature Review
0064	Normal	Research on sustainable networking converges on a clear starting point: network infrastructure has historically been optimized for connectivity and performance, while energy use was treated as a secondary concern. Green networking emerged to address that gap by asking how routing, topology management, and equipment states can be adjusted to reduce power consumption without destabilizing service quality (Bianzino et al., 2012; Gupta & Singh, 2003). That framing is directly relevant to GreenNet, whose central problem is not generic AI optimization but energy-aware control under networking constraints.
0065	Normal	Classical routing protocols explain why this gap exists. OSPF, for example, is a link-state protocol that computes forwarding from link costs and topology advertisements; it does not include native objectives for sleeping links, reducing device power, or reasoning about carbon impact (Moy, 1998). GreenNet therefore treats traditional routing as an operational baseline: forwarding remains shortest-path based, while energy-aware behavior is introduced at the controller layer rather than by redefining the control plane itself.
0066	Normal	Green-networking studies show that traffic consolidation and selective deactivation of underutilized resources can reduce power consumption, particularly in backbone-style settings where parts of the network are overprovisioned for peak demand (Chiaraviglio et al., 2009; Gupta & Singh, 2003). At the same time, survey work emphasizes that these gains are only meaningful when they are balanced against packet delivery, latency, and resilience requirements (Bianzino et al., 2012). GreenNet adopts exactly that trade-off perspective: turning links off is useful only when the resulting QoS cost stays within a defensible bound.
0067	Normal	Machine learning adds a different research strand. Work such as Valadarsky et al. (2017) shows that learned approaches can be used to search for strong routing configurations, while reinforcement learning provides the general decision-making framework for balancing long-term reward against immediate penalties (Sutton & Barto, 2018). GreenNet uses that literature selectively. The project does not attempt end-to-end learned path computation; instead, it applies reinforcement learning to link-state control over a fixed routing baseline, which is a narrower and more defensible problem formulation for a capstone-scale system.
0068	Normal	Simulation methodology is equally important. Network simulators are valuable because they allow repeated experiments under controlled conditions, but the right level of fidelity depends on the research question. For GreenNet, the priority is not full protocol emulation; it is transparent comparison among controllers, fast iteration over seeds and scenarios, and direct integration with Python-based evaluation code. That is why the project uses a lightweight graph-based simulator rather than a heavier discrete-event platform, while still following the networking-research principle that simulation assumptions should be explicit and experiments repeatable (Henderson et al., 2008).
0069	Normal	The sustainability context keeps these technical choices from becoming purely local optimizations. The IEA (2022) notes that data centres and data transmission networks are material contributors to digital-sector electricity use, which means even modest efficiency gains matter when they can be justified and reproduced. In GreenNet, sustainability is therefore treated as an engineering objective that must coexist with reliability and QoS, not as a rhetorical add-on to a generic AI project.
0070	Normal	A practical gap remains between the literature and accessible research tooling. Many publications present optimization results, but fewer provide an integrated environment in which topologies, scenarios, controller outputs, evaluation thresholds, stored artifacts, and reviewer-facing visualizations are all connected. GreenNet is designed to fill that methodological gap by combining controller comparison, reproducible storage, and reporting into one thesis-scale system.
0071	Normal	The literature therefore informs GreenNet in three concrete ways: it motivates energy-aware control as a legitimate networking problem, it justifies comparing learned and non-learned controllers, and it supports the thesis decision to judge success through controlled benchmarking instead of isolated favorable runs.
0073	Heading 2	Methodology
0074	Normal	This thesis uses a design-and-evaluation methodology in which GreenNet itself is the primary research instrument. The work proceeds by implementing the simulator and controller stack, defining controlled comparison rules, executing repeated experiments, and interpreting outcomes against explicit hypothesis thresholds rather than against narrative preference, in line with simulation-centered networking research practice (Henderson et al., 2008).
0075	Normal	At the methodological core is a configurable Python-based environment that separates topology generation, traffic generation, routing baseline selection, controller logic, and metric collection. Simulation is used because it allows the same policies to be replayed across multiple seeds and scenarios with stable assumptions about capacity, latency, power, and carbon intensity. For this thesis, that level of control is more useful than attempting direct deployment on live infrastructure (Henderson et al., 2008).
0076	Normal	The network model represents the system as a graph of nodes and links with per-edge capacity, latency, utilization, and active-state information. Traffic is injected as flows under canonical scenarios such as normal, burst, and hotspot conditions, and each step records delivered traffic, dropped traffic, average delay, path latency, energy use, and carbon-related output. This creates a shared measurement space in which sustainability and QoS can be evaluated together rather than in separate chapters.
0077	Normal	Controller comparison is then performed over the same simulator. The All-On controller keeps every controllable link active, the heuristic controller applies conservative link-state rules, and the policy labeled PPO in the benchmark represents a PPO-based hybrid controller whose learned action proposals are filtered by masking, cooldowns, and recovery logic. This distinction is important because the thesis evaluates the implemented GreenNet controller stack, not an abstract pure-PPO baseline detached from the codebase.
0078	Normal	Experiment execution is organized around reproducible artifacts. Runs emit per-step metrics, summaries, environment configurations, metadata, and aggregated reports, and the project also persists structured outputs through SQLite-backed storage. This means that the thesis can discuss not just what result was obtained, but which scenario, seed, and controller settings produced it.
0079	Normal	Interpretation is governed by an explicit acceptance gate. In the final evaluation logic, success requires a substantial energy reduction together with bounded QoS degradation: delivered-traffic loss must remain within 2%, dropped-traffic increase within 5%, delay and path-latency increase within 10%, and QoS-violation-rate increase within 0.02. That threshold-based methodology prevents modest energy gains from being overstated as full hypothesis confirmation.
0080	Normal	Finally, the methodology includes reviewer-facing reporting. GreenNet aggregates run data into comparison tables, stored summaries, and interface views so that policy trade-offs can be inspected from the same evidence used in the written analysis. The method is therefore both computational and documentary: it is designed to produce results and to make those results auditable.
0082	Heading 2	System Architecture
0083	Normal	GreenNet is organized as a modular research stack whose components map directly to the tasks of simulation, control, evaluation, storage, and reporting. The architecture is deliberately layered so that a change in controller logic does not require rewriting the simulator, and a change in reporting does not alter the underlying benchmark rules.
0084	Normal	The simulation core is responsible for the network state itself. It maintains a graph of nodes and links, tracks active and inactive edges, computes path allocations under the configured routing baseline, enforces capacity constraints, estimates delay from utilization and base latency, and converts active-state decisions into energy and carbon-related metrics. This is the component that turns controller actions into measurable networking outcomes.
0085	Normal	Traffic enters the simulator through a separate generation layer. GreenNet supports scenario-driven demand patterns rather than a single fixed trace, including calmer normal conditions and more stressed burst or hotspot conditions. The environment can also attach lightweight online forecasting features, but these forecasts are used as runtime signals inside the controller loop, not presented as a stand-alone predictive thesis contribution.
0086	Normal	Control logic sits above that network state. GreenNet keeps forwarding behavior on a static shortest-path or OSPF-like baseline and varies only the controller responsible for link-state decisions. That separation is architecturally important because it lets the thesis compare All-On, heuristic, and PPO-based control without implying that path computation itself is being reinvented by the AI layer.
0087	Normal	The evaluation layer transforms raw step data into thesis-level evidence. It aggregates totals and means for energy, delivery, drops, delay, path latency, QoS violation rate, and carbon-related output, then feeds those summaries into benchmark tables and acceptance-threshold checks. Without this layer, the simulator would only produce logs; with it, GreenNet produces interpretable comparisons.
0088	Normal	Reproducibility depends on the persistence layer. GreenNet stores run metadata, environment configuration, summaries, and per-step measurements so that a benchmark can be revisited after the fact. That storage is not incidental bookkeeping. It is what allows the thesis to distinguish official benchmark artifacts from exploratory runs and to defend why some figures remain in the main body while others are moved to the appendix.
0089	Normal	A backend API layer connects the stored and computed results to the interface. In practice, this service exposes run lists, summaries, aggregate comparisons, and simulator-facing metadata so that the reporting surface is driven by the same data used in the written analysis.
0090	Normal	The visualization layer is the final architectural component. GreenNet uses a web interface to inspect topology state, controller comparisons, and result summaries, which makes the framework useful for thesis defense and review as well as for internal experimentation. The interface is therefore part of the research workflow: it helps translate benchmark artifacts into inspectable evidence.
0091	Normal	Architecturally, GreenNet is best understood as a comparative experimentation platform. Its value comes from the way the simulator, controllers, metrics, storage, and interface reinforce one another to support reproducible claims about energy-aware networking.
0093	Heading 2	Implementation
0094	Normal	GreenNet was implemented as a working software system rather than as a paper design. The completed project includes the simulator, controller stack, experiment runners, persistence layer, backend API, and user-facing visualization needed to execute and interpret comparative benchmarks.
0095	Normal	The simulator implementation models the network as a graph with per-edge capacity, latency, utilization, and active-state attributes. At each step it resolves active routing paths, allocates flow demand subject to capacity limits, measures delivered and dropped traffic, computes delay and path-latency values from congestion-sensitive formulas, and derives energy and carbon-related outputs from a configurable power model.
0096	Normal	Controller implementation is intentionally separated from forwarding. The All-On controller leaves the topology fully active, the heuristic controller uses explicit rules to toggle edges conservatively, and the benchmark policy labeled PPO combines learned PPO proposals with masks, cooldowns, and safety overrides. That implementation detail matters to the thesis because the final AI result should be interpreted as constrained hybrid control, not as unconstrained end-to-end learned routing.
0097	Normal	GreenNet also implements an experiment pipeline around those controllers. Command-line runners execute individual scenarios, matrix-style evaluations, and final aggregation steps; results are written as structured CSV and JSON artifacts and can be reloaded later for comparison or reporting. This pipeline is what makes the thesis benchmark reproducible rather than a one-off demonstration.
0098	Normal	Metric collection and persistence are built into execution rather than added afterward. Per-step and aggregated values for energy, carbon, delivered traffic, dropped traffic, average delay, path latency, and controller activity are stored so that summaries, acceptance checks, and figures all derive from the same evidence trail.
0099	Normal	The implementation also includes the reporting stack. FastAPI-based endpoints expose runs and summary data, while the frontend presents topology playback, policy comparisons, and result views. This is important for GreenNet because the project is intended to be reviewed as a complete comparative framework, not merely as backend code that produces opaque files.
0100	Normal	Several auxiliary capabilities were added carefully rather than overclaimed. The environment includes lightweight online forecasting options such as EMA, Holt-style trend estimation, and adaptive EMA ensembles, and the codebase contains an exploratory impact-predictor path. In the final thesis framing, however, these features support the controller environment; they are not treated as separately validated headline contributions.
0101	Normal	Implementation therefore serves the central thesis purpose directly: it turns the question of energy-aware control into a benchmarkable system with reproducible artifacts, comparable controller outputs, and explicit QoS-constrained interpretation.

0124	Heading 2	Challenges and Limitations
0125	p1	Developing GreenNet required balancing networking realism, ML integration, and capstone-scale feasibility. Many of the thesis decisions that make the project manageable also define the limits within which its results should be interpreted.
0126	p1	The first limitation is the simulator itself. GreenNet models forwarding and link-state control over a graph abstraction; it does not emulate a full routing control plane, device firmware behavior, or operational maintenance workflows. That is why the thesis treats the routing baseline as shortest-path or OSPF-like forwarding behavior rather than as a complete implementation of OSPF in production conditions.
0127	p1	Taken together, Figures 1 and 2 support a precise interpretation. The aggregate benchmark shows that the heuristic controller remains the strongest overall policy and that the hypothesis is not achieved. The case study shows that the AI-based controller is not decorative; it can produce beneficial trade-offs under selected conditions. The correct academic conclusion is therefore mixed: GreenNet demonstrates a real but limited AI signal inside a stronger comparative framework whose main contribution is methodological.
0128	p1	Another important challenge was maintaining the balance between energy savings and Quality of Service. It is relatively easy to reduce simulated energy consumption if a system aggressively disables links, concentrates traffic without sufficient safeguards, or ignores performance degradation. The real difficulty lies in reducing energy usage without causing unacceptable levels of packet drops, excessive delay, instability, or QoS violations. This trade-off is central to the project and also one of its biggest design challenges. Any energy-aware system can appear successful if it is judged only by lower consumption, but such an evaluation would be misleading. For this reason, GreenNet had to be designed around multi-metric comparison, where performance-related indicators remain just as important as sustainability-related ones.
0129	p1	The AI layer introduces an additional honesty constraint. In the final evaluation path, the policy labeled PPO is not a pure unconstrained learner; it operates inside a safety-oriented wrapper that uses masks, cooldowns, and fallback logic. This makes the controller more practical for the benchmark, but it also means that any observed gain cannot be attributed to raw PPO alone.
0130	p1	Training depth is another limitation. Reinforcement learning typically benefits from long tuning cycles and broad scenario exposure, yet GreenNet was developed within academic time bounds. The final PPO-based controller should therefore be read as a time-bounded thesis implementation, not as an upper bound on what reinforcement learning could achieve in a larger study.
0131	p1	Traffic modeling was another limitation in the project. GreenNet supports scenario-based experimentation through synthetic or controlled traffic patterns, which is useful for reproducibility and structured comparison. However, synthetic traffic can only approximate the complexity of real network demand. Real traffic often contains irregular bursts, long-range temporal dependencies, application-specific behavior, and patterns driven by human activity or infrastructure events that are difficult to capture fully in a simplified simulator. Because of this, GreenNet can demonstrate how routing policies behave under representative conditions, but it cannot claim to replicate the full unpredictability of operational Internet-scale environments.
0132	p1	Topology realism presents a similar issue. The project is able to model different network structures and compare policy behavior across them, but these topologies are still simplified representations. Real backbone, ISP, enterprise, or data-center networks often include additional constraints such as routing policy rules, heterogeneous hardware capacities, resilience requirements, maintenance windows, and operational restrictions. A research project at this scale cannot fully replicate all such conditions. This means that the architectural and policy insights produced by GreenNet are valuable for analysis and experimentation, but they should not be interpreted as complete substitutes for real network engineering validation.
0133	p1	From a software-engineering perspective, integration was itself a challenge. The simulator, controller logic, benchmark scripts, storage layer, API, and frontend all share assumptions about metric names, policy labels, and run metadata. Keeping those layers aligned was necessary for reproducibility, but it also required disciplined refactoring and careful artifact management throughout the project.
0134	p1	Project scope imposed a final boundary. GreenNet prioritizes simulation-based comparison, locked evaluation artifacts, and reviewer-facing reporting over production deployment concerns such as distributed orchestration or direct control of physical routers. That trade-off is deliberate and appropriate for the thesis question.
0135	p1	These limitations do not weaken the thesis contribution; they define it. GreenNet is valuable precisely because it states its assumptions, keeps its benchmark claims conservative, and makes clear where future work would be needed before any result could be generalized beyond the simulated environment.
0137	Heading 2	Evaluation Strategy
0138	Normal	The evaluation strategy of GreenNet is built around one question: under the same simulated conditions, which controller produces the most acceptable trade-off between energy reduction and service quality? Every part of the benchmark design follows from that question, and that trade-off framing is consistent with the green-networking literature on balancing efficiency against service quality (Bianzino et al., 2012; Chiaraviglio et al., 2009).
0139	Normal	The working expectation for GreenNet was that adaptive control would reduce energy use relative to the All-On controller while staying within acceptable QoS bounds. The final benchmark partially supports that direction of travel, but it does not deliver the level of improvement required for hypothesis confirmation.
0140	Normal	Scenario diversity is the second principle. GreenNet does not evaluate a controller on a single happy-path workload; it uses at least normal, burst, and hotspot conditions to expose how each policy behaves under calm demand, fluctuating demand, and more stressed traffic. That matters because a controller that appears efficient in one regime may fail or become too conservative in another.
0141	Normal	The strategy is also explicitly multi-metric. Energy reduction is recorded alongside delivered traffic, dropped traffic, average delay, path latency, QoS violation rate, and carbon-related output. This prevents a controller from being judged successful merely because it turns more links off.
0142	Normal	Reproducibility is treated as part of evaluation rather than as an afterthought. GreenNet stores environment configurations, run metadata, summaries, and supporting artifacts so that benchmark claims can be revisited later. In a project with stochastic traffic and a learned controller, that storage discipline is essential.
0143	Normal	Aggregation is the fourth principle. The thesis does not rely on a single illustrative run; it summarizes outcomes across policies, scenarios, and repeated executions, then derives final comparisons from those aggregates. This is what allows the project to distinguish a broadly stable trend from an isolated favorable example.
0144	Normal	Most importantly, GreenNet uses threshold-based interpretation. The final hypothesis gate requires strong energy improvement together with bounded QoS change, so a controller can remain 'acceptable' in QoS terms and still fail to achieve the thesis hypothesis. That stricter standard is why the final benchmark is interpreted conservatively rather than advertised as a success on the basis of a small energy gain.
0145	Normal	This evaluation strategy answers the research questions directly. It tests whether adaptive control can save energy, whether the AI-based controller improves on non-AI baselines in a meaningful way, and whether the framework itself can support repeatable sustainable-networking research.
0146	Normal	In short, GreenNet evaluates policies by the quality of their trade-offs and by the reproducibility of the evidence behind those trade-offs.

0167	p1	This thesis presented GreenNet as a reproducible framework for studying energy-aware network control under explicit QoS constraints. The project combined a graph-based simulator, controller comparison, artifact-backed evaluation, persistent storage, and interface-level reporting into one coherent system for sustainable-networking experiments.
0169	Heading 2	What Was Achieved
0170	p1	GreenNet met its core implementation goals. The project delivered a configurable simulator for topology and traffic experimentation, a controller stack containing All-On, heuristic, and PPO-based hybrid policies, a benchmark pipeline that stores summaries and run metadata, and a web-facing reporting layer that makes results inspectable. Those pieces are sufficient to support serious comparative analysis rather than only a prototype demonstration.
0171	p1	From a research perspective, the strongest conclusion is not that AI won, but that the comparison is now defensible. The heuristic controller remains the best overall policy in the final benchmark, while the PPO-based controller is the strongest AI-based policy and shows limited but genuine promise in selected conditions such as the flash-crowd case study. That is a useful outcome because it identifies both the current ceiling of the implementation and the conditions under which adaptive control may become more worthwhile.
0173	Heading 2	Why the Hypothesis Was Not Fully Met
0174	p1	The working expectation for GreenNet was that adaptive control would reduce energy use relative to an All-On configuration while staying within acceptable QoS bounds. The final benchmark partially supports that direction of travel, but it does not deliver the level of improvement required for hypothesis confirmation.
0175	p1	Several factors explain that result. The simulator uses a deliberately simplified but explicit power model; the PPO-based controller is constrained by masks and safety logic; reward shaping penalizes QoS degradation strongly; and the benchmark topologies remain modest in size. Together, these design choices encourage conservative behavior, which is useful for stability and honesty but limits the magnitude of achievable energy savings.
0176	p1	Seen in that light, the negative result is informative rather than disappointing. It shows that under GreenNet’s current assumptions, small AI-driven improvements are possible, but strong claims of sustainable-routing optimization would be premature.
0178	Heading 2	On the Importance of Honest Research Reporting
0179	p1	An important strength of the thesis is that it reports the benchmark on its own terms. The acceptance logic is capable of rejecting an attractive narrative, and in this case it does: the final result is partial, not triumphant. That is exactly the kind of discipline a comparative systems thesis should demonstrate.
0180	p1	This matters especially in AI-oriented networking work, where it is easy to highlight a single favorable run or to blur the distinction between a pure learned controller and a safety-constrained hybrid system. GreenNet avoids that by keeping policy labels, benchmark thresholds, and supporting artifacts visible throughout the analysis.
0182	Heading 2	Future Work
0183	p1	Future work should extend GreenNet in directions that are directly suggested by the benchmark. The most immediate step is deeper controller training and clearer ablation between the learned PPO component and the surrounding safety logic so that future gains can be attributed more precisely.
0184	p1	A second step is evaluation on larger and more heterogeneous topologies with more realistic demand traces. If energy-aware control is to matter beyond the capstone scale, it must be tested in settings where path diversity, redundancy, and load variation create more room for meaningful savings.
0185	p1	Further work could also strengthen the project’s auxiliary components without overstating them. Forecasting and impact-prediction paths should be evaluated separately if they are to be claimed as substantive contributions, and benchmark documentation should continue to prioritize artifact lineage and threshold-based interpretation.
0187	Heading 2	Closing Remarks
0188	p1	GreenNet’s central contribution is therefore methodological and practical: it provides a principled environment in which sustainable-networking ideas can be tested, compared, and reported honestly. The final benchmark is intentionally modest, but the framework itself is strong enough to support more ambitious future studies without requiring the thesis to exaggerate what has already been achieved.
